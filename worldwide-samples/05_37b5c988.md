# U.S. and Iran negotiate 60‑day cease‑fire extension amid frozen‑asset dispute

*Talks focus on a 14‑point memorandum, release of Iranian funds and reopening the Strait of Hormuz as hostilities pause.*

`Hybrid A (try 1) · 656 words · verify=pass`

---

The conflict that began on 28 February with joint United States‑Israel strikes on Iran entered a new phase after a cease‑fire was brokered on 8 April. Since then, negotiators from Washington and Tehran have moved toward a tentative 60‑day memorandum of understanding that would extend the fragile pause in fighting and lay out a framework for broader settlement.  

Iran’s side submitted a 14‑point proposal to the Pakistani mediation team, outlining sanctions relief, frozen‑asset arrangements, nuclear negotiations and maritime security. The draft memorandum is based on a comparable 14‑point U.S. plan for ceasing hostilities. Both parties have signaled willingness to adopt the 60‑day timetable, which would “pause, or stop” the clock on combat operations, according to senior adviser Hegseth.  

A central point of contention is the release of Iranian funds held abroad. Tehran has demanded the immediate unlocking of between $12 billion and $24 billion in frozen assets, while also seeking a fraction of its roughly $100 billion total frozen holdings once an interim deal is signed. The United States, in turn, has asked for $20 billion to be released as part of the broader package.  

“The negotiations are proceeding in an orderly and constructive manner, and I have informed my representatives not to rush into a deal, in that time is on our side,” President Donald Trump said, adding that the naval blockade would remain “in full force and effect until an agreement is reached, certified, and signed.”  

U.S. officials stress that the agreement would guarantee long‑term regional peace, curb Iranian funding of violence and secure a robust inspection regime. “It guarantees a long‑term peace in the region, and that would mean, of course, that the Iranians are no longer funding violence in the region,” a White House official said.  

Iran’s demands also include the removal of all naval mines within 30 days and the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz, a chokepoint through which roughly 20 percent of the world’s oil and gas supply normally passes. The memorandum would therefore have significant implications for global energy markets; Brent crude fell to about $89 a barrel, down 4.4 percent on the day, as traders priced in the prospect of reduced tension.  

Casualties have risen sharply during the 40‑day combat period. Iranian authorities report 3,375 deaths from U.S. and Israeli strikes. The Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps also claimed to have destroyed a U.S. MQ‑1 Predator drone operating over international waters.  

Financially, the Pentagon disclosed that the United States has spent an estimated $29 billion on the war, with a separate cost estimate of $25 billion for the campaign.  

Pakistan’s prime minister, Shehbaz Sharif, announced that a U.S.–Iran peace deal could be finalized within 24 hours, underscoring the regional urgency of a resolution.  

U.S. Senator JD Vance expressed optimism about the potential outcome. “I feel that we are in a position to get a deal that is good for the United States economically, and that really does deal with the Iranian nuclear program… for the long term, to where my kids can say when they’re adults, Iran is not going to have a nuclear weapon,” he said.  

Israeli officials, while not party to the memorandum, welcomed the prospect of an agreement that would include “the removal of enriched material, the dismantling of the enrichment infrastructure, limits on missile production and the cessation of Iran’s support for its terrorist proxies,” according to a statement from Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu’s office.  

The next steps hinge on whether the parties can bridge the gap over frozen assets and verification mechanisms within the 60‑day window. If talks falter, Pentagon officials have warned that the United States stands ready to resume strikes, a stance that reflects the war‑fighting authority granted to the president for up to 60 calendar days unless Congress consents to an extension.  

For now, the cease‑fire holds, the Strait of Hormuz remains open, and the world watches as diplomatic efforts attempt to translate a 14‑point draft into a durable peace.
