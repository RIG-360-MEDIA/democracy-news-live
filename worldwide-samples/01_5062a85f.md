# SpaceX’s record‑breaking IPO raises $75 billion, values firm at about $1.75 trillion

*The Texas‑based rocket and AI company began trading on Nasdaq on June 12 after selling roughly 555 million shares at $135 each.*

---

SpaceX, the Starbase, Texas‑based aerospace and artificial‑intelligence firm founded by Elon Musk, completed an initial public offering that raised $75 billion, the largest ever for a single listing. The company set a final price of $135 per share and offered about 555.6 million shares, putting its market valuation at roughly $1.75 trillion as trading opened on Nasdaq on Friday, June 12.  

The IPO proceeds were drawn from a broad set of financial firms, with the company reporting that the $75 billion raised came entirely from the public offering. In its prospectus, SpaceX disclosed that the deal could allocate as much as 30 % of the shares to individual investors, a move that underscored the high demand from both institutional and retail participants.  

At the $135 price point, the offering placed SpaceX among the most valuable U.S.‑listed companies. Analysts noted that the valuation sits within a projected range of $1.7 trillion to $2 trillion, making it the sixth‑largest U.S.‑listed firm by market capitalisation, with a post‑IPO market cap reported at approximately $2.1 trillion. The company’s shares opened at $150, briefly exceeding the set price before stabilising near the offering level.  

Musk retained decisive control of the company after the float. He will hold 82.4 % of the voting power and retain a 42 % equity stake, while his personal wealth, now anchored largely by SpaceX, rose to over $1.1 trillion, making him the first person in history to surpass a $1 trillion net worth. The bulk of his wealth is tied to a stake in SpaceX valued at roughly $866 billion.  

Financial metrics released alongside the IPO paint a mixed picture of the firm’s recent performance. SpaceX reported revenue of $18.7 billion for the prior year, while posting a net loss of $4.94 billion. Over the twelve‑month period ending March 31, 2026, the company’s cumulative loss widened to $8.7 billion.  

The IPO also highlighted SpaceX’s broader strategic ambitions. In a company statement, SpaceX said that “by dramatically reducing the cost of access to space, we have been able to expand our mission to address some of the Earth’s most pressing challenges, including bridging the digital divide by aiming to connect over three billion unconnected people to the internet and humanity’s collective knowledge.”  

Industry observers offered varied assessments of the listing’s pricing. Reuters noted the “record $75 billion” raised, while analyst Ishan Tanna warned that “at a $1.75 trillion valuation, investors are betting more on future execution than current fundamentals,” describing the stock as potentially risky for those chasing post‑IPO momentum. Michael Field echoed a more cautious stance, calling the valuation “extremely speculative” given the “unknown and untested technologies” underpinning the AI and satellite businesses.  

Despite the cautionary notes, some commentators highlighted the scale of the capital raise. Dan Coatsworth described SpaceX as “not only a record breaker in terms of money raised at a stock market debut, but it has also left other big names for dust,” noting the firm’s starting valuation “pushing $2 trillion.”  

The IPO also triggered operational changes on the exchange. Nasdaq Inc. recently amended its listing rules, allowing SpaceX to qualify for the Nasdaq 100 Index within 15 trading days, a move that could further increase the stock’s visibility among passive funds.  

SpaceX’s partnership ecosystem expanded alongside the offering. Google agreed to a monthly infrastructure payment of $920 million, underscoring the synergy between the rocket company’s satellite network and the search giant’s data‑center ambitions.  

The public offering concluded with a market capitalisation of approximately $2.2 trillion, according to post‑trading data, cementing SpaceX’s position as one of the world’s most valuable publicly listed enterprises. As the company moves forward, its ability to translate the massive valuation into sustainable earnings will remain a focal point for investors and analysts alike.
