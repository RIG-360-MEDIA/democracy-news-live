# Federal Judge Declares Trump’s $100,000 H‑1B Fee Unlawful

*A U.S. district court ruled the fee lacks congressional authority, overturning the policy that charged foreign workers $100,000 for expedited processing.*

A federal judge in Washington, D.C., on Monday ruled that the $100,000 fee imposed by the Trump administration on new H‑1B visa petitions for highly skilled foreign workers is unlawful. The court found that the fee functions as a tax on H‑1B petitions and was enacted without the delegation required by Congress.  

The $100,000 surcharge was introduced as part of a broader effort by the Trump administration to curb what officials described as “abuse” of the H‑1B program. The policy promised applicants that payment would accelerate processing to roughly 15 days, compared with the typical 7.5‑month timeline for standard applications.  DHS Secretary Markwayne Mullin testified before a Senate panel that more than 200,000 applicants had paid the fee, allowing their cases to be “processed in about 15 days.”  

Despite the promise of speed, the fee saw limited uptake. U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services (USCIS) recorded only 85 payments of the $100,000 charge. In the fiscal year 2026, USCIS received about 2.86 lakh (286,000) H‑1B applications, but properly submitted registrations fell by 38.5 percent compared with the previous year. Industry observers noted that the steep cost discouraged many prospective applicants, contributing to a near 40 percent decline in H‑1B registrations projected for fiscal year 2027.  

The court’s decision was anchored in the principle that only Congress may levy taxes. The judge, identified in court filings as Leo Sorokin, concluded that the fee “imposes a tax on H‑1B petitions without the requisite delegation by Congress,” rendering the policy invalid. The ruling also highlighted that the fee effectively barred citizens of 39 countries—spanning Africa, Asia, Latin America and the Middle East—from receiving final immigration decisions, a practice the court deemed beyond the agency’s authority.  

Legal challenges to the fee had been mounted by business groups, including the U.S. Chamber of Commerce, which argued that the surcharge exceeded the agency’s statutory mandate. Following the decision, the administration’s attempt to codify the fee through legislation was referenced by Republican Representative Mike Kennedy, who said, “An activist judge stepped in to strike down the President's critical H‑1B immigration reforms. The judge claims only Congress can do this? Fine. I already introduced the PROTECT Act, which will codify the President's H‑1B reforms and make it the law of the land.”  

The ruling is expected to have immediate operational effects. USCIS will no longer collect the $100,000 surcharge, and the expedited processing track tied to the payment is presumed to be discontinued. Applicants who previously paid the fee may need to refile under the standard processing timeline, extending the average wait from 15 days to the usual 7.5 months.  

The decision also revives broader debates over the H‑1B program’s structure. Critics of the visa lottery argue that it enables employers to replace American STEM workers with cheaper foreign labor, while supporters contend that the program fills essential skill gaps, particularly in sectors such as healthcare. The administration had introduced a separate “H‑1Bs for Physicians and the Healthcare Workforce Act” aimed at addressing physician shortages, but the fee’s removal removes a financial lever that some lawmakers believed could prioritize domestic workers.  

For now, the court’s order restores the pre‑fee status quo, leaving the H‑1B system to operate under existing statutory limits of 65,000 visas per year, with 70 percent of those visas historically held by Indian nationals. The long‑term impact on the program’s demand and on U.S. employers’ hiring strategies will likely unfold as the immigration community adjusts to the absence of the $100,000 expedited track.
